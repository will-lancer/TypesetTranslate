Although the states of non-zero energy form bose-fermi pairs
as indicated in equation (31), this is certainly not true for the
states of zero energy. Any zero energy state, boson or fermion, is
just annihilated by $Q$:
\begin{equation}
Q|b, E = 0\rangle = Q|f, E = 0\rangle = 0
\tag{33}
\end{equation}
Again, this is so because $Q^2 = \frac{1}{2} H$. The zero energy states are
singlets --- one dimensional representations of supersymmetry.

The general form of the spectrum of a supersymmetric theory is
indicated in figure (9). The states of non-zero energy are in bose-fermi
pairs. The zero energy states are not paired in general since
each one is separately annihilated by $Q$. They need not be equal in
number. In the figure there are two boson states of zero energy,
and one fermion.

Conversely, as we change the parameters, a zero energy
state may get a non-zero energy. If so, as soon as it gets non-zero
energy, it must have a partner (figure (11)), because states
of non-zero energy are always in bose-fermi pairs. It is not possible
however, for a zero-energy state to simply appear or disappear.
In quantum mechanics states always move around continuously in
energy.\begingroup\renewcommand{\thefootnote}{\fnsymbol{footnote}}\footnotemark[1]\endgroup

In the process of figures (10) and (11), the number of zero
energy states changes. However, the difference between the number
$n_B^{E=0}$ of zero energy states that are bosons and the number $n_F^{E=0}$
that are fermions does not change. This will be our basic tool.

Formally, the difference $n_B^{E=0} - n_F^{E=0}$ may be interpreted as
the trace of the operator, $(-1)^F$, that distinguishes bosons from
fermions. Formally, the states of non-zero energy cancel out of the
trace of $(-1)^F$ because they come in bose-fermi pairs. The trace of
$(-1)^F$ can therefore be evaluated among the zero energy states only,
and equals $n_B^{E=0} - n_F^{E=0}$. We will henceforth refer to $n_B^{E=0} - n_F^{E=0}$
as $\mathrm{tr}(-1)^F$. However, this is only a definition, since $\mathrm{Tr}(-1)^F$ is
not absolutely convergent.

The fact that $\mathrm{Tr}(-1)^F$ does not change when the parameters of a
supersymmetric theory are changed is an important fact, for the following
reasons:

(1) If $\mathrm{Tr}(-1)^F \neq 0$, supersymmetry is definitely not spontaneously broken.

(2) $\mathrm{Tr}(-1)^F$ can be calculated reliably even in quite complicated theories.

Let me explain these points:

If $\mathrm{Tr}(-1)^F \neq 0$ then $n_B^{E=0} \neq 0$ or $n_F^{E=0} \neq 0$ or both. In any case,
there are some zero energy states. Hence the ground state energy is
zero and supersymmetry is unbroken. (Since $\mathrm{Tr}(-1)^F$ is independent of
the volume, a non-zero value of $\mathrm{Tr}(-1)^F$ means that the ground state
energy is zero for any $V$ and hence also as $V \to \infty$.)

$\mathrm{Tr}(-1)^F$ can be calculated reliably because it is independent of
the parameters. We can calculate $\mathrm{Tr}(-1)^F$ in some convenient limit,
such as small volume, large bare mass, and weak coupling. Almost any
theory simplifies enough in this limit (or some analogous limit) that
$\mathrm{Tr}(-1)^F$ can be calculated reliably.

The results can then be applied to the situation of interest ---
large volume, physical mass, and physical coupling --- because $\mathrm{Tr}(-1)^F$
is independent of all parameters.

It is important to realize that $\mathrm{Tr}(-1)^F$ can be calculated
reliably even though, in general, we may be unable to tell which
states have exactly zero energy. Suppose that in some approximation
we find in some theory the spectrum of figure (12(a)). In this approximation
we have $n_B^{E=0} = 2$, $n_F^{E=0} = 1$, and $\mathrm{Tr}(-1)^F = 1$. Even if
the approximation is excellent we cannot be sure we have calculated
$n_B^{E=0}$ or $n_F^{E=0}$ correctly. As a result of small errors in any approximation,
the true answer might be that of figure (12(b)). This corresponds
to $n_B^{E=0} = 1$, $n_F^{E=0} = 0$, and again $\mathrm{Tr}(-1)^F = 1$. The hypothetical
corrections to our approximation gave a small, non-zero
energy to one boson and therefore (by supersymmetry) also to one
fermion. The original approximation gave $n_B^{E=0}$ and $n_F^{E=0}$ incorrectly,
but it gave $\mathrm{Tr}(-1)^F$ correctly, essentially because the extra boson at
$E = 0$ has no potential fermion partner and so no way to get $E \neq 0$.
